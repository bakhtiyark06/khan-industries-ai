# Khan Industries — Sales & Client Success Manual

This document is the **single source of truth for how Khan Industries acquires clients, runs consultations, creates proposals, hands off to delivery, and grows long-term partnerships**.

It governs every founder, sales contributor, consultant, and AI assistant involved in revenue motion — from the first website lead through post-delivery retention. This is an **internal operating manual** — not marketing copy. Customer-facing materials must be derived from this manual and sibling KOS documents, not the other way around.

If a sales or client success decision conflicts with this manual, **this manual wins** until it is deliberately updated.

> Khan Industries does not sell software — it **solves business problems**. Technology is the mechanism; business outcomes are the goal. Every conversation, proposal, and follow-up must prove that conviction.

**Current implementation status:** Khan Industries is an early-stage company with founder-led sales, no deployed CRM product, and no documented client case studies yet. Standards in this manual describe the **target sales practice**, with **phased adoption** where noted. Do not claim win rates, pipeline metrics, or client logos that do not exist.

**Relationship to `03_SERVICES.md`:** `03_SERVICES.md` owns service delivery from signed scope through long-term partnership. This manual owns everything from **website lead capture** through **signed proposal and sales-to-delivery handoff**, plus retention and expansion motion that sales and client success lead.

## 1. Sales Philosophy

Khan Industries sells **business outcomes**, not technology stacks. The sales motion exists to help the right customers achieve measurable value — save time, increase revenue, reduce operational complexity, improve customer experience, and scale intelligently (`00_COMPANY.md`).

**Four sales convictions:**

1. **Outcomes over features.** Prospects do not buy APIs, agents, or frameworks. They buy relief from pain and progress toward goals. Lead every conversation with the outcome, then explain the mechanism.
2. **Trust over transactions.** A signed deal that damages trust is a loss, not a win. The relationship outranks the quarter.
3. **Consultation over pitching.** Khan Industries advises before it recommends. Discovery and honest guidance are the product of the first conversation.
4. **Partnership over projects.** The default relationship is long-term — retainers, product subscriptions, and repeat engagements — not one-off delivery that ends at deployment.

**The sales test:** before any proposal is sent, ask: *Does this engagement clearly map to at least one of the five value outcomes, and can Khan Industries deliver it with premium quality at the stated scope?* If either answer is no, do not send the proposal.

**Canonical funnel** (aligned with `AGENTS.md` and `02_PRODUCTS.md` §13):

```
Portfolio / Products / Solutions (discovery)
    ↓
Lead (identified interest)
    ↓
Consultation (qualified conversation)
    ↓
Proposal (scoped engagement)
    ↓
Client (signed + delivery begins — handoff to 03_SERVICES)
    ↓
Long-term Partnership (retainer, products, expansion)
```

## 2. Sales Principles

Sales Philosophy states *why* Khan Industries sells differently. **Sales Principles** are timeless beliefs that guide every conversation — not tactics, scripts, or pipeline mechanics. When the playbook does not cover an edge case, resolve the decision using these principles in order of relevance.

Each principle is documented in three parts: **why it exists**, **how it influences sales decisions**, and **how it supports brand and long-term relationships**.

---

### 2.1 Sell Business Outcomes, Not Technology

**Why it exists.** Khan Industries exists to create practical business value (`00_COMPANY.md`). Technology is how value is delivered, not what customers are buying. Leading with features trains prospects to compare vendors on specs instead of outcomes.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Open discovery with business pain and success criteria, not a product tour. Frame proposals around measurable outcomes (hours saved, tickets deflected, leads captured) before listing deliverables. Decline engagements where the prospect only wants a cheap technical task with no clear business case.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Premium positioning (`01_BRAND.md`) is earned by speaking the customer's language — revenue, operations, stress, quality — not engineering jargon. Clients who buy outcomes stay when technology changes; clients who buy buzzwords churn when the next trend arrives.

---

### 2.2 Listen Before Recommending Solutions

**Why it exists.** Trust before transaction is a core customer experience principle (`01_BRAND.md` §13). Recommending before understanding is presumptuous and often wrong — it signals sales pressure, not partnership.

**How it influences sales decisions.** The first half of every consultation is questions, not answers. No solution is named until business context, constraints, and success criteria are documented. If the prospect asks "what do you sell?" early, redirect: *"I'd like to understand your situation first so I can recommend something that actually helps."*

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Clients feel heard, not sold to. Recommendations that follow deep listening have higher acceptance rates and lower scope disputes because they match real need.

---

### 2.3 Trust Is More Valuable Than Short-Term Revenue

**Why it exists.** Integrity is a core company value (`00_COMPANY.md`). Chasing revenue from bad-fit clients, overscoped deals, or dishonest positioning destroys the brand faster than any slow quarter.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Walk away from deals that require overselling, unrealistic timelines, or capabilities Khan Industries does not have. Accept smaller, well-scoped Starter engagements over oversized proposals that cannot be delivered. Prefer a respectful "not a fit" over a signed contract that will fail.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Every declined bad-fit deal protects future referrals and reputation. Trust compounds; short-term revenue from a failed engagement does not.

---

### 2.4 Never Oversell Capabilities

**Why it exists.** The honesty policy in `AGENTS.md` and product status rules in `02_PRODUCTS.md` exist because overstated capability is indistinguishable from fraud. AI agents especially invite hype; Khan Industries must be the calm, accurate voice.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Label every product honestly: Concept, Beta, or Launched. Demo only what works today. State assumptions, exclusions, and dependencies in proposals. If engineering cannot meet a timeline (`05_ENGINEERING.md`), sales does not promise it.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Understated capability that delivers creates the closing feeling: *"I made the right decision choosing Khan Industries."* (`01_BRAND.md` §13). Oversold capability creates refunds, disputes, and negative word of mouth.

---

### 2.5 Recommend Only Solutions That Genuinely Solve the Customer's Problem

**Why it exists.** The company decision-making principle (`00_COMPANY.md`) asks whether an engagement saves time, makes money, reduces stress, improves quality, or makes systems easier to run. Selling something that does not pass this test violates company purpose.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Use the Product vs Service Decision Matrix (Section 15) honestly — including recommending a competitor, a simpler DIY path, or no purchase when appropriate. Do not bundle services or products the client does not need to inflate deal size.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Clients remember vendors who told them the truth. A smaller correct engagement often becomes a larger partnership when trust is established.

---

### 2.6 Build Long-Term Partnerships Instead of Chasing One-Time Projects

**Why it exists.** The funnel end state is long-term partnership (`AGENTS.md`). Services and products are designed to begin relationships, not end them at deployment (`03_SERVICES.md` §1).

**How it influences sales decisions.** Propose retainers, product subscriptions, and phased roadmaps where appropriate. Discuss post-launch support and success reviews during the sales process, not after delivery. Avoid positioning engagements as "build and disappear" unless the client explicitly wants that and it is documented.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Recurring revenue and referrals come from clients who succeed over years. One-time project chasing optimizes for margin today and churn tomorrow.

---

### 2.7 Educate Before Persuading

**Why it exists.** Khan Industries voice is consultative and precise (`01_BRAND.md` §5). Hype, pressure tactics, and manufactured urgency violate brand rules (§15). Educated buyers make better clients and fewer disputes.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Explain trade-offs, risks, and realistic timelines before asking for commitment. Share relevant documentation (product status, process overview) proactively. Never use fake scarcity, countdown timers, or "limited spots" in sales conversations.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Clients who understand what they bought are satisfied clients. Education builds authority; persuasion tricks build skepticism.

---

### 2.8 Underpromise and Overdeliver

**Why it exists.** Delivery that exceeds the brochure is a customer experience principle (`01_BRAND.md` §13). Engineering Definition of Done (`05_ENGINEERING.md` §3) requires realistic scope. Proposals that promise the maximum possible create delivery failure.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Scope proposals with explicit assumptions and exclusions. Add buffer to timelines for unknowns. Promise Starter-tier depth at Starter scope — not Enterprise breadth. Celebrate delivery that exceeds written scope as a surprise, not a baseline.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Consistent over-delivery builds the premium reputation Khan Industries depends on. Over-promising trains clients to expect miracles and review negatively when reality arrives.

---

### 2.9 Protect the Khan Industries Brand in Every Interaction

**Why it exists.** Brand rules (`01_BRAND.md` §15) protect trust across every surface — website, proposals, email, calls, and demos. Sales is often the first human touchpoint; it sets the standard for everything that follows.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Match brand voice in every email and proposal: calm, professional, precise. Use correct naming, formatting, and tier language. Do not use hype words, unverifiable claims, or casual tone that undermines premium positioning. Treat every prospect interaction as if it will be forwarded to their executive team.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Consistent premium execution across touchpoints signals that delivery will match. Inconsistent sales experience creates doubt before the contract is signed.

---

### 2.10 Leave Every Customer Better Than You Found Them, Even If They Never Become a Client

**Why it exists.** Integrity over conversion (`00_COMPANY.md`). Not every prospect is a client; every prospect is a potential referral source, future client, or public voice about Khan Industries.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Provide useful guidance even when declining a deal. Share relevant resources, honest assessments, or referrals when appropriate. Close lost deals respectfully with a clear reason and an open door. Never ghost, pressure, or belittle a prospect who says no.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Prospects who were treated well return when timing changes and refer others. Prospects who were pressured become cautionary stories.

---

### 2.11 Solve the Customer's Real Business Problem, Not Just the Solution They Initially Ask For

**Why it exists.** Prospects often arrive with a predetermined solution ("we need a chatbot") when the real problem is different (support volume, knowledge gaps, process failure). AI Consulting and discovery discipline exist to diagnose before prescribing.

**How it influences sales decisions.** Challenge solution requests respectfully: *"Help me understand why a chatbot — what problem are you trying to solve?"* Recommend a smaller scope, a different service, or a product instead of custom build when that serves the client better. Document the diagnosed problem separately from the requested solution in CRM and handoff notes.

**How it supports brand and long-term relationships.** Clients hire Khan Industries for judgment, not order-taking. Solving the real problem creates measurable success; building the wrong solution creates shelfware.

## 3. Customer Success Philosophy

Sales does not end at signature. Customer success means the client **achieves the business outcomes** the engagement was designed for — not merely that a project shipped (`03_SERVICES.md` §10).

**Sales and client success share these convictions:**

1. **Outcomes over outputs.** A deployed system nobody uses is a failure regardless of invoice paid.
2. **Proactive communication.** Clients should never wonder what is happening — during sales, onboarding, and delivery.
3. **Education empowers.** Training and documentation reduce dependency and increase satisfaction (Section 28).
4. **Honest measurement.** Review success metrics with the client where data exists — without inventing results.
5. **Expansion when earned.** Upsell only when it clearly serves stated client goals (Section 31).

**The closing standard** from `01_BRAND.md` §13 applies to every stage:

> *"I made the right decision choosing Khan Industries."*

Sales sets the expectation; delivery and client success fulfill it.

## 5. Sales Values and Non-Negotiables

Sales applies company values (`00_COMPANY.md`) with zero compromise:

| Value | Sales expression |
| --- | --- |
| **Quality** | Every proposal, email, and demo reflects premium craft — even at Starter scope |
| **Integrity** | Honest capability, status, and timelines; no fake social proof |
| **Innovation** | Recommend modern solutions when they serve the client — not hype for its own sake |
| **Trust** | Do what was promised; say what is true |

**Non-negotiables:**

- Never invent clients, testimonials, revenue figures, win rates, or pipeline metrics.
- Never optimize short-term revenue over trust (Principle 2.3).
- Never send a proposal without documented discovery and value-outcome mapping.
- Never mark a deal Won without a signed agreement and handoff package (Section 27).
- Never separate ethics from quota — Section 39 rules apply at every company stage.

## 7. Lead Qualification

Qualification determines whether Khan Industries invests consultation time. Use a **value-fit framework** adapted for early-stage consultative sales — not rigid BANT gatekeeping.

### 7.1 Qualification Criteria

| Criterion | Question | Pass signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Problem clarity** | Is there a real business pain? | Prospect can describe impact on time, revenue, stress, quality, or operations |
| **Outcome fit** | Does the need map to Khan Industries capabilities? | Aligns to services (`03_SERVICES.md`) or products (`02_PRODUCTS.md`) |
| **Value alignment** | Will success be measurable? | Agreed success criteria are achievable |
| **Budget realism** | Can they invest in premium work? | Not seeking cheapest vendor; understands value framing |
| **Timeline** | Is there a reasonable urgency? | Defined need; not infinite deferral |
| **Decision access** | Can the prospect decide or strongly influence? | Decision-maker on call or clearly identified |
| **Integrity fit** | Is this an honest engagement? | No pressure for fake claims, gray-area use cases, or deceptive AI |

### 7.2 Qualification Outcomes

| Outcome | Action |
| --- | --- |
| **Qualified** | Schedule consultation; record in CRM |
| **Nurture** | Not ready now — add to nurture cadence (Section 25) |
| **Declined** | Respectful close with reason — refer elsewhere if helpful |
| **Refer** | Need is real but not Khan Industries fit — maintain goodwill (Principle 2.10) |

### 7.3 Disqualification Criteria

Decline or defer when:

- The prospect requires capabilities Khan Industries does not have and cannot honestly acquire in timeline.
- The engagement would require overselling product status or fake case studies.
- Budget expectations are incompatible with premium delivery (seeking cheapest bid only).
- The use case violates company values (deceptive, harmful, or surveillance technology).
- The prospect will not provide access to decision-makers or basic discovery information.
- Scope is undefined and the prospect refuses discovery — "just send a price" without context.

**Principle:** declining a bad-fit lead is a sales success, not a failure.

## 8. Lead Stages and Sales Pipeline

### 8.1 Pipeline Stages

| Stage | Definition | Exit criteria |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **New** | Lead captured | Contact info + source recorded |
| **Qualified** | Fit confirmed | Passes Section 7 framework |
| **Consultation Scheduled** | Meeting booked | Calendar confirmed; agenda sent |
| **Consultation Completed** | Discovery done | Problem + outcomes documented |
| **Proposal Sent** | SOW/proposal delivered | Written scope + tier labeled |
| **Negotiation** | Active discussion | Objections addressed honestly |
| **Won** | Signed agreement | Handoff initiated (Section 27) |
| **Lost** | Declined or unfit | Reason recorded; respectful close |
| **Nurture** | Not now, maybe later | Follow-up cadence defined |

### 8.2 Stage Rules

- Every lead must have a stage and a **next action** with a date.
- Deals should not sit in a stage beyond agreed aging limits (see Section 10).
- Stage regression is allowed when new information changes fit (e.g., Consultation Completed → Lost).
- **Won** requires signed agreement — verbal yes is Negotiation until paperwork is complete.

### 8.3 Pipeline Hygiene

- Weekly review at growth stage (Section 10).
- No orphan leads without owner (founder at early stage).
- Lost and Nurture records kept for learning — not deleted.

## 11. Customer Discovery Process

Discovery collects the information needed to recommend honestly and scope accurately.

### 11.1 Discovery Domains

| Domain | Sample questions |
| --- | --- |
| **Business context** | What does the company do? Who are their customers? |
| **Pain** | What is not working today? What triggered this inquiry? |
| **Impact** | What does this cost in time, money, stress, quality, or growth? |
| **Current systems** | What tools, workflows, and data exist today? |
| **Constraints** | Budget range, timeline, compliance, team capacity, technical limits |
| **Success criteria** | What does "solved" look like in 30, 90, 180 days? |
| **Decision process** | Who decides? What is their timeline? Other vendors? |
| **Requested vs real problem** | What did they ask for vs what they actually need? (Principle 2.11) |

### 11.2 Discovery Outputs

Before recommending a solution, document:

1. **Problem statement** — in the customer's words, refined for clarity
2. **Value outcome mapping** — which of five outcomes apply
3. **Constraints and assumptions**
4. **Success metrics** — measurable where possible
5. **Fit assessment** — qualified, nurture, decline, or refer

Discovery notes feed proposals and the handoff package (Section 27).

## 12. Consultation Framework

### 12.1 Purpose

The consultation is a **structured discovery conversation** — consultative, not a product pitch. Goal: determine whether Khan Industries can create real value and what engagement shape fits.

### 12.2 Format

| Element | Standard |
| --- | --- |
| **Duration** | 30–45 minutes (Starter/SMB); 45–60 minutes (Growth/Enterprise) |
| **Attendees** | Prospect decision-maker or strong influencer; Khan Industries founder or AE |
| **Medium** | Video preferred; phone acceptable |
| **Recording** | Only with explicit consent |

### 12.3 Consultation Agenda Template

```
1. Welcome and context (2 min)
   - Confirm time; set agenda; no pitch promise

2. Prospect situation (15–20 min)
   - Business, pain, impact, current systems
   - Success criteria and constraints
   - Listen — do not interrupt with solutions

3. Clarifying questions (5–10 min)
   - Real problem vs requested solution
   - Decision process and timeline

4. Initial observations (5–10 min)
   - Honest assessment of fit
   - High-level direction only — not full proposal
   - Product status disclosed if relevant

5. Next steps (5 min)
   - Proposal, nurture, decline, or refer — stated clearly
   - Timeline for follow-up
   - Questions from prospect
```

### 12.4 Consultation Outputs

| Output | Owner | Destination |

## 13. Business Problem Analysis

Before any solution recommendation, map the diagnosed problem to the **five value outcomes** (`00_COMPANY.md`):

| Outcome | Diagnostic questions |
| --- | --- |
| **Save time** | Where are people doing repetitive manual work? |
| **Increase revenue** | Where are leads, conversions, or revenue leaking? |
| **Reduce operational complexity** | Where are errors, handoffs, or chaos creating stress? |
| **Improve customer experience** | Where are customers waiting, confused, or underserved? |
| **Scale intelligently** | What breaks if volume doubles? |

**Document the analysis** in discovery notes before Section 14 recommendations. If no outcome maps clearly, pause — the engagement may not be worth pursuing.

## 14. Solution Recommendation Framework

### 14.1 Recommendation Order

1. Confirm diagnosed problem and outcomes (Section 13).
2. Apply Product vs Service Decision Matrix (Section 15).
3. Select engagement tier (Section 17).
4. State honest product status for any product included (`02_PRODUCTS.md`).
5. Outline phased approach if full scope exceeds Starter tier.
6. Disclose what Khan Industries will **not** include.

### 14.2 Recommendation Types

| Type | When | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Service only** | Custom need, unique workflow | Business Automation + API Development |
| **Product only** | Standard repeatable need; product Launched or Beta | SupportFlow AI subscription |
| **Product + service** | Product needs deployment, integration, or customization | SupportFlow AI + AI Agent Development |
| **Consulting first** | Unclear scope; strategy needed before build | AI Consulting → phased proposal |
| **No engagement** | Bad fit or prospect served better elsewhere | Respectful decline (Principle 2.10) |

### 14.3 Honest Status Language

| Status | Sales language |
| --- | --- |
| **Concept** | "In development; available for early access, pilot, or co-development — not production-ready off the shelf." |
| **Beta** | "Working product in limited release; suitable for early adopters with agreed support terms." |
| **Launched** | "Production-ready per product standards; standard subscription or deployment terms apply." |

Never use Launched language for Concept products.

## 15. Product vs Service Decision Matrix

Implements the rule from `02_PRODUCTS.md` §12: **products are scalable and repeatable; services are custom and hands-on**.

| Customer need | Recommend | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Standard support automation, repeatable | Product (when Launched) or product-led service deploy | SupportFlow AI |
| Highly custom integration, unique workflow | Service | Business Automation + API Development |
| Website credibility + lead gen | Service (Website Development) + optional SiteScope | SiteScope → website upsell path |
| Exploratory AI strategy | Service (AI Consulting) | May lead to phased build |
| Ongoing optimization | Retainer (Growth/Enterprise) | Post-project |
| Prospect wants "AI" without clear use case | Service (AI Consulting) first | Diagnose before build |
| Mature product fits with light config | Product + minimal service | SupportFlow deploy package |

**Decision rule:** if the solution must be invented from scratch for one client, it is a service. If the same core system serves many clients with configuration, it is a product.

Products and services **reinforce** each other (`02_PRODUCTS.md` §12) — they must never compete for the same positioning in a proposal.

## 16. Pricing Philosophy

Sales applies pricing philosophy from `01_BRAND.md` §14 and `03_SERVICES.md` §6. This section governs **how price is discussed and framed** — not specific dollar amounts.

**Core rules:**

- **Value-based framing.** Tie price to outcomes — time saved, revenue enabled, complexity reduced.
- **Transparent structure.** Proposals list included, excluded, and change-order triggers.
- **Never cheapest.** Compete on quality, reliability, and trust.
- **Every tier is premium.** Starter is scoped premium work, not discount quality.
- **Products vs services.** Recurring product pricing per `02_PRODUCTS.md`; custom services per engagement proposal.

**In conversations:**

- Discuss investment range after discovery — not before understanding need.
- Avoid hourly-rate shopping as the primary frame.
- No fake discounts, hidden fees, or manufactured urgency (`01_BRAND.md` §15).

## 17. Starter, Growth, and Enterprise Engagements

Aligned with `01_BRAND.md` §14 and `03_SERVICES.md` §6.

| Tier | Who it serves | Qualification signals | What sales promises |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Starter** | SMB, first engagement | Single primary deliverable; focused scope; defined boundaries | Premium quality at tight scope; clear timeline; explicit exclusions |
| **Growth** | Scaling businesses | Multiple deliverables; integrations; phased work; retainer interest | Deeper systems; ongoing improvement path; structured checkpoints |
| **Enterprise** | Large or complex organizations | Compliance, security, multi-team stakeholders; strategic partnership | Tailored architecture; rigorous process; dedicated attention; documented SLAs as agreed |

**Sales rules:**

- Label the tier in every proposal.
- Do not sell Starter scope at Growth price without documented scope expansion.
- Enterprise engagements require technical validation before proposal (Section 38 — growth stage).

## 18. Proposal Creation Standards

### 18.1 Proposal Purpose

A proposal is a **formal statement of scope, outcomes, timeline, assumptions, and investment** — not a marketing brochure.

### 18.2 Proposal Structure Outline

```
1. Executive summary
   - Client situation (brief)
   - Recommended approach
   - Expected outcomes (mapped to value outcomes)

2. Scope of work
   - Services and/or products included
   - Deliverables list
   - Engagement tier (Starter / Growth / Enterprise)

3. Out of scope / exclusions
   - Explicit list — prevents scope creep disputes

4. Assumptions and dependencies
   - Client responsibilities (content, access, decisions)
   - Third-party tools or accounts

5. Timeline and milestones
   - Realistic per 05_ENGINEERING and 03_SERVICES
   - Checkpoint schedule

6. Investment
   - Value-framed pricing
   - Payment terms
   - Change order process

7. Product status disclosure (if applicable)
   - Concept / Beta / Launched labels

8. Next steps
   - Signature process
   - Kickoff timeline
```

### 18.3 Proposal Quality Bar

- [ ] Discovery notes support every scoped item
- [ ] Success criteria stated
- [ ] Tier labeled
- [ ] Assumptions and exclusions explicit
- [ ] Timeline reviewed against engineering realism
- [ ] Product status honest
- [ ] Brand voice: consultative, precise (`01_BRAND.md` §5)
- [ ] No fake case studies or unverifiable claims

### 18.4 Change Orders

Scope changes after signature follow `03_SERVICES.md` — written change order or revised proposal. Sales does not promise post-signature work verbally without documentation.

## 19. Objection Handling Principles

Objections are requests for clarity, not attacks. Handle with honesty and calm confidence.

| Objection type | Principle | Response approach |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Price too high** | Value framing | Return to outcomes; compare to cost of unsolved problem; never race to bottom |
| **Timeline too long** | Underpromise | Explain what realistic delivery includes; offer phased delivery if appropriate |
| **Need to think about it** | No pressure | Agree; offer summary email; set follow-up date (Section 25) |
| **Competitor comparison** | No trash talk | Compare on fit, honesty, and outcomes — not insults |
| **AI skepticism** | Educate | Transparent disclosure; bounded capability; human escalation |
| **Company too small / early-stage** | Honesty | Acknowledge stage; emphasize standards, craft, and founder attention |
| **Want cheaper vendor** | Integrity | Confirm premium positioning; decline if only price matters |

**Never:** fake scarcity, false deadlines, or pressure closes.

Khan Industries may have **no client case studies yet**. This is acceptable. Trust is built by:

- Delivering premium consultation experience
- Following through on every commitment
- Never inventing logos, testimonials, or metrics

When case studies exist (Section 35), they strengthen trust — they are not required to sell honestly today.

- Premium presentation: stable connection, professional backdrop, clear audio.

## 29. Customer Lifecycle

Post-delivery lifecycle stages for account management:

```
Hypercare (0–30 days post-launch)
    ↓
Active (healthy engagement)
    ↓
At-risk (signals of dissatisfaction or disuse)
    ↓
Champion (advocate, referral source, expansion ready)
```

| Stage | Signals | Sales/CS action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Hypercare** | Recent launch | Proactive check-ins; ensure adoption |
| **Active** | Regular use; positive communication | Scheduled value reviews |
| **At-risk** | Missed milestones; complaints; disuse | Escalate; remediation plan; no upsell |
| **Champion** | Strong results; willing to refer | Referral ask (Section 33); expansion when earned |

## 30. Customer Retention

Retention is cheaper and more valuable than acquisition.

**Practices:**

- **30-day post-launch review** for Growth and Enterprise (`03_SERVICES.md` §10.2)
- **Value reviews** — what metrics moved, what friction remains
- **Proactive support** — do not wait for complaints
- **Honest reporting** — share what is and is not working

Retention failures often begin in sales — oversold scope, wrong fit, unclear success criteria. Section 7 qualification and Section 18 proposals are retention investments.

---

## 31. Upselling and Cross-selling Principles

Aligned with `03_SERVICES.md` §10.1: **expansion when earned**.

**Appropriate when:**

- Client achieved stated outcomes from prior engagement
- New need maps clearly to value outcomes
- Client explicitly requests expansion
- Product subscription adds measurable value (e.g., SupportFlow after custom agent)

**Inappropriate when:**

- Client is at-risk or dissatisfied
- Upsell serves Khan Industries revenue but not client goals
- Pressure tactics or artificial urgency
- Bundling unrelated services to inflate deal size

**Cross-sell examples (when earned):**

- SiteScope AI audit → Website Development
- Custom agent → SupportFlow AI subscription for maintenance
- Website project → Business Automation for lead routing

## 33. Referral Strategy

Referrals are **earned**, not bought.

**Rules:**

- Ask only after documented success and client satisfaction
- Never incentivize fake reviews or misleading testimonials
- Make referral easy: clear description of ideal referral, simple intro process
- Thank referrers regardless of outcome

**Early stage:** founder network and genuine relationships are primary referral sources until case studies exist.

## 34. Long-Term Partnership Philosophy

The funnel end state is **long-term partnership** — not a completed project file.

**Partnership means:**

- Ongoing value through products, retainers, or repeat projects
- Honest communication over years
- Shared success metrics reviewed periodically
- Khan Industries grows as the client grows

**Sales implication:** every engagement should be structured with a path to partnership — even Starter projects — without forcing retainers that deliver no value.

## 39. What Khan Industries Sales Will Never Do
